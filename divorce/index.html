Divorce & Separation

This page is for families living through separation, divorce, and the changes that follow. It’s not about perfect co-parenting. It’s about helping kids feel safe, loved, and not responsible for adult problems.

Core message: Kids don’t need you to “be okay.” They need you to be steady. When adults stay calm and clear, children feel less alone inside the change.

A 60-second reset

If your body is in “fight/flight,” it’s harder to respond the way you want. Try this once.

Inhale 4… exhale 6… (twice).
Then name 3 things you can see.

Most kids ask the same silent questions: “Is it my fault?” “Who will take care of me?” “Do I still belong?” Your job isn’t to answer everything, it’s to keep the message consistent: You're loved. You’re safe. You’re not the cause.

  • Keep adult details away from children (money, blame, infidelity, court).
  • Try one predictable routine that stays the same in both homes (bedtime phrase, calendar, check-in text).
  • Expect “waves”, regression, mood swings, clinginess, anger, acting-out, stomach aches.
  • When you mess up: repair quickly (“I’m sorry. That wasn’t about you.”).

Little kids (approx. 3–7)

Short, concrete words. Reassurance. Repetition. Routine.

School age (approx. 8–12)

More questions, loyalty conflicts, fairness worries.

Teens (approx. 13–18)

Privacy + autonomy. Strong opinions. Hidden sadness.

All ages

Signs it may help to add extra support.

Transitions between homes can be the hardest part; not because kids don’t love both parents, but because switching can spike stress.

  • Preview: “Tonight is Dad’s night. Tomorrow is Mom’s night.”
  • Bridge object: a small item that travels (stuffie, photo, keychain).
  • Neutral handoff: keep it brief, kind, and adult-only.
  • After-drop reset: snack + water + quiet time before homework/talk.
Small but powerful: Make a shared calendar visible to the child/teen (even a simple one on the fridge). Predictability reduces anxiety.

What kids need to hear most

“This is not your fault.” “Both parents love you.” “You don’t have to take sides.” “You’re allowed to love both homes.” “It’s okay to miss the way things were.”

One line to keep repeating: Kids do best when they don’t have to carry adult feelings.

When things feel messy

Separation can bring grief, anger, relief, guilt, and fear, and sometimes all in one day. If your reactions feel bigger than you want, that’s a sign your nervous system is overwhelmed, not that you’re failing.

  • Pause before texting back.
  • Write the message, wait 10 minutes, then edit it.
  • Choose “child-focused” language over “winning.”

Safety note

If you or your child is experiencing intimidation, threats, stalking, or violence, your safety comes first. Consider reaching out to local supports, a trusted professional, or emergency services if needed.

Canada: If you’re in immediate danger, call 911. For mental health crisis support, call or text 988.
